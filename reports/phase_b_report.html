01

Context and Objectives

Phase A delivered a working risk model (19 features, ROC-AUC 0.779) that flags clients likely to become chronically homeless at the moment of intake. Phase B had two objectives:

  1. Extend Model 1 with service features already present in the CAM data — specifically the client's housing type and first intervention at intake — to improve predictive accuracy.
  2. Answer PECH's original question: which interventions are associated with avoiding chronic homelessness, particularly among clients the model identifies as high-risk?
Design constraint

All new features are derived from the client's first snapshot only, preserving the no-leakage guarantee of the Phase A model. The model still predicts risk at the moment of intake using only information available on day one.

Selection bias — important

Chronic rates differ widely across interventions, but this reflects who gets assigned each intervention, not whether the intervention works. SPDAT (71%) and Case Management (48%) are assigned to the most vulnerable clients from the start. The model accounts for this through the mean risk score — not through raw chronic rates.

What the metrics mean in plain terms

The test set contains 2,349 clients, of whom 421 actually became chronic (17.9%). Here is what each metric means in concrete terms at threshold 0.20:

Metric What it measures In practice (Phase B)
ROC-AUC 0.874 If you pick one chronic client and one non-chronic client at random, how often does the model rank the chronic one higher? 87.4% of the time the model correctly ranks the higher-risk client above the lower-risk one.
Recall 0.983 Of all clients who actually became chronic, how many did the model flag? Of the 421 truly chronic clients, 414 are correctly flagged. Only 7 slip through undetected.
Precision 0.377 Of all clients the model flags as high-risk, how many actually became chronic? The model flags ~1,098 clients as high-risk. Of those, 414 truly become chronic and ~684 are false alarms — caseworkers review them and no action is needed.
F1 0.545 Balanced average of recall and precision. Improves from 0.459 (Phase A) to 0.545 — both recall and precision moved in the right direction together.
The recall–precision trade-off

Setting the threshold low (0.20) casts a wide net: the model catches 98.3% of truly chronic clients, at the cost of ~6 in 10 alerts being false alarms. Raising the threshold would reduce false alarms but miss more real cases.

For this use case — social services where missing a high-risk client is more costly than a false alarm — high recall is the right priority. A caseworker can review an alert that turns out to be unnecessary; they cannot help a client who was never flagged.

The housing features are the dominant driver of the Phase B lift. last_known_housing_category is the single most predictive feature in the model — consistent with Phase A's finding that housing stability is the strongest predictor of chronic homelessness. The intervention feature (first_intervention_type) contributes meaningfully but ranks lower, in part because it captures assignment patterns (who gets which intervention) rather than intervention effects.

Key patterns

Goods and Services signal

+9 percentage points at intake (28.9% vs 19.9%) and +11 points at last recorded intervention (31.3% vs 20.2%) in clients who never became chronic. This is the most consistent signal across both intake and trajectory analyses. Mean risk score for Goods and Services clients is 0.712 — substantially lower than Case Management (0.869), but still in the high-risk band.

Case Management and selection bias

Case Management appears much more frequently in clients who did become chronic (25.7% vs 19.0% at intake; 16.6% vs 6.1% at last intervention). This is consistent with selection bias: caseworkers assign intensive case management to the most vulnerable clients, who have the highest initial risk regardless of the intervention. Mean risk score for Case Management clients is 0.869 — the highest of all interventions.

Clients who became chronic had more snapshots and more intervention changes — consistent with longer, more complex cases. The "never chronic" group left the system faster and with fewer touchpoints.

Most common intervention transitions

In high-risk clients who never became chronic, the most frequent consecutive intervention pairs are:

  • Admissions → Goods and Services (88 instances)
  • Admissions → Group Activities (58 instances)
  • Goods and Services → Admissions (53 instances)

In clients who did become chronic, the same transitions appear but at much higher volume, with Case Management transitions becoming far more prominent (Admissions → Case Management: 355 instances).

06

Limitations and Honest Caveats

  • Precision remains below target. At threshold 0.20, precision is 0.377 — below the 0.50 target. Roughly 6 in 10 clients flagged as high-risk did not become chronic in the test set. The model is designed for recall (catching true positives), but caseworkers should be aware of the false alarm rate.
  • Training/test distribution shift. The training set has a 39.3% chronic rate vs 17.9% in the test set. This reflects real temporal change in the population — newer clients may have shorter follow-up or differ in profile. Performance should be monitored over time.
  • Intervention outcomes are descriptive only. We cannot establish causal effectiveness of any intervention from observational CAM data. The Goods and Services signal is consistent and noteworthy, but it requires a controlled study to validate.
  • Geographic features excluded. Geographic region has 47–69% missing data and was excluded from both phases. If location correlates with access to services, this is an uncontrolled confound.
  • External validity. The model was trained on Waterloo Region data. Performance may differ in other systems with different intake processes or population profiles.
⚠ Fairness alert — Youth group

The model performs significantly worse for clients under 25 years old. At threshold 0.32, the recall gap between age groups is 0.401 — well above the 0.10 alert threshold.

Age group N (test set) Recall False Negative Rate Status
Youth (<25) 466 0.551 44.9% ALERT
Adult (25–55) 1,511 0.877 12.3% PASS
Senior (>55) 372 0.952 4.8% PASS

Of every 10 young clients who will actually become chronic, the model only flags 5 — the other 5 leave intake without being identified as high risk. This gap needs to be investigated before relying on the model for decisions involving youth clients. A possible cause is that young clients have different patterns of housing instability and intervention that the current features do not capture well.
